Course Recap · 70-387

What this course set out to do, and why.

The hope in this (disjointed) semester is that you built a way of thinking about markets that most business professionals never develop. Here is what that looks like.

Learning Objectives
01

Formulate platform strategy

Design strategies to grow, maintain, and capture value from digital platforms, including launch sequencing, monetization timing, and governance design.

02

Apply strategic frameworks

Use management concepts like network effects, multi-homing costs, and tipping conditions to explain how and when platforms grow and decline.

03

Analyze competitive dynamics

Distinguish between inter-platform competition (which ecosystem wins), intra-platform competition (who wins within an ecosystem), and platform envelopment.

04

Communicate strategic decisions

Critically analyze real-world platform events and articulate strategic reasoning clearly, both in writing and in discussion.

Why does this matter?

Every sector in Qatar National Vision 2030, from logistics to education to healthcare, is a platform opportunity. The frameworks you developed this semester are not abstract. They are the strategic vocabulary for the economy you are about to enter. Companies that understand network effects, governance tradeoffs, and platform launch dynamics will shape Qatar's digital infrastructure. The ones that don't will supply it.

Topic 01 · Network Effects

The value of a platform is not what it does. It is who else is on it.

Concept

Same-side vs. cross-side, strong vs. weak

Cross-side: more riders make Uber more valuable to drivers. Same-side: can be positive (messaging apps) or negative (too many sellers competing for the same buyers). The strength of NEs determines whether markets tip.

From the cases

Fasten had the model. It didn't have the network.

Lower commissions and transparent pricing weren't enough when riders couldn't get a car in 4 minutes. Network effects don't reward fairness. They reward density.

Exercise · Multiple Choice
A food delivery app in Doha has 200 restaurants but very few customers ordering. Adding 50 more restaurants does not increase orders. Why?
✓ Correct. More restaurants don't help if no one is ordering. Cross-side NEs require both sides to reach a threshold. This is the chicken-and-egg problem in action: supply without demand is just a catalogue.
Think about which side creates value for which. Adding restaurants only matters if someone is there to order from them. What needs to happen on the demand side before supply-side growth starts compounding?
Topic 02 · Platform Launch

Every platform starts with zero users on both sides. How you solve that defines everything.

Concept

Eight strategies, one problem

Seeding, piggybacking, marquee, single-side, micro-market, big-bang, producer evangelism, side-switching. Each solves the chicken-and-egg differently. The right strategy depends on which side is harder to attract and what assets you already have.

From the cases

Alfie needed trainers before it could attract trainees

Without credible fitness professionals on the platform, no consumer would sign up. Alfie's launch challenge was a supply-first problem: the product was only as good as who was already on it.

Topic 03 · Monetization

Charge the side that can bear it. Subsidize the side that makes you valuable.

Concept

Price asymmetrically, and time it right

Subsidize whoever is harder to attract or more valuable to have. Charge whoever values their presence on the other side. Charging too early, before lock-in, kills the network effect you haven't grown yet.

From the cases

Spotify and ZBJ: same logic, different contexts

Spotify kept listeners cheap (freemium) to build the audience that gave it leverage with labels. ZBJ had to build transaction trust before taking a meaningful cut. The principle is the same: monetize after lock-in, not before.

Exercise · Multiple Choice
You're launching a tutoring marketplace in Qatar. Students are price-sensitive; tutors invest significant time setting up profiles. What's the right approach at launch?
✓ Right. Students are elastic, they'll leave if you charge them early. Tutors invest in setup and stay once they have bookings. Charging tutors before you can deliver volume is a quick way to have zero tutors. Free-for-now also signals confidence that you'll earn the right to charge later.
Think about which side is more price-sensitive and which side creates value for the other by being present. The monetization logic follows from those two questions, not from who "benefits most."
Topic 04 · Platform Design

Pull, facilitate, match. In that order, always.

Concept

Three functions, three failure modes

Pull: attract both sides. Facilitate: reduce friction in the exchange. Match: connect the right participants. Most platforms fail at one while over-investing in the others.

From the cases

Airbnb's discrimination problem was a facilitate failure

Airbnb could pull hosts and guests and match them well, but hosts were rejecting guests with ethnic-sounding names. The platform connected the parties but couldn't remove bias from the transaction itself.

Topic 05 · Governance

Platforms write the rules. Enforcing them on your most valuable participants is the hard part.

Concept

Openness is a dial, and every position has a tradeoff

More open = more innovation, more fragmentation, lower margins. More closed = quality control, higher margins, less external innovation. The right level depends on what you're optimizing for. There's no neutral setting.

From the cases

Epic vs. Apple: a fight about terms, not platforms

Epic wasn't building a rival to iOS. It was contesting the terms of participation inside Apple's ecosystem. The anti-steering ruling broke Apple's information asymmetry: developers could now tell users about cheaper alternatives, even without off-platform payment.

No. Our concerns about big tech firms misusing user data is NOT the same as turning on a camera in a Zoom session! 😊

Exercise · Multiple Choice
A top-rated consultant on your platform starts steering clients to pay via bank transfer, bypassing your 15% fee. Three clients have agreed. What's the strategically sound response?
✓ Governance credibility requires that rules apply to popular participants, especially popular ones. A private warning signals that the rules are negotiable for high earners. Every other consultant on the platform is watching this call.
Think about what your response signals to every other consultant. Rules that bend for popular participants aren't rules; they're suggestions with a VIP exemption. What does that do to your platform's governance credibility long-term?
Topic 06 · Competition

Inter-platform: which ecosystem wins. Intra-platform: who wins within the ecosystem.

Concept

Two different wars, two different logics

Inter-platform: competing to be the ecosystem. Tipping logic applies: strong NEs + costly multi-homing + no niching = winner-take-all. Intra-platform: competing within an ecosystem. Platform controls distribution and sets the terms.

From the cases

Lasso (Facebook's clone of TikTok) failed; Reels survived, because of envelopment

Lasso required TikTok users to adopt a new app from zero. Reels was embedded in Instagram's 1B+ user base. Meta didn't need to win a platform adoption battle; it just needed Reels good enough that users wouldn't bother switching.

Exercise · Multiple Choice
Talabat adds a grocery delivery tab inside its existing app, directly competing with a standalone grocery delivery startup in Doha. What type of competitive threat is this?
✓ Envelopment. Talabat bundles grocery delivery into its existing app, leveraging its installed base and logistics relationships. The standalone startup has to build its network from scratch. It can't match Talabat's reach without matching its network, and matching the network takes years.
Key question: is Talabat competing against another platform to win users, or is it using its existing platform to absorb a neighboring market? The latter is envelopment, one of the most lethal moves in platform competition.
